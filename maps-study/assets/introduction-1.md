# Can you use choropleth maps to interpret spatial patterns?

A **Bivariate choropleth Map** (shown above) uses color to represent two (2) event rates associated with geographical regions (e.g, counties or states). For example, a county with 80 out of 100 people who consume a product (X) and 90 out of 100 who consume a product (Y) is shaded in dark red (A), while a county with  15 out of 100 people who consume a product (X) and 10 out of 100 who consume a product (Y) is shaded in light grey (B). 

We can visually identify numerous patterns using bivariate choropleth maps, such as regions with high consumption of product (X) and product (Y) (shaded in dark red) or regions with low consumption of product (X) and product (Y) (shaded in light grey).

In this study, you will be asked to use a bivariate map to answer questions about how you interpret and make sense of patterns shown.
